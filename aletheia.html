Negative to Print

Complete film pipeline. Expose onto negative, process through dye chemistry, print to final stock.

Input Space

Declares camera color space and transfer function. Supports ARRI LogC3/4, Sony S-Log3, RED Log3G10, Blackmagic Film Gen5, DaVinci Wide Gamut, ACES AP1/cct, and Rec.709. Linearization and gamut conversion happen before any film math.

Negative Stock

42 motion picture and still negative stocks with per-stock H&D curves derived from Kodak and Fuji datasheets. Each stock has unique gamma, density range, dye spectral response, and color separation behavior.

Dye Process

10 dye coupling processes—Kodak Dye Transfer, Technicolor 3-Strip and Monopack, Ilford Ilfochrome. Models how silver halide converts to dye during development. Each process has distinct saturation curves and color crosstalk.

Push/Pull

Simulates over or under-development in stops. Push increases contrast and grain, pull softens. Adjusts exposure compensation to maintain mid-gray while changing shoulder and toe behavior.

Bleach Bypass

Skips the bleach step—retained silver produces desaturation and increased contrast. The look made famous by Saving Private Ryan and Se7en.

Interlayer Effect

Authentic emulsion layer interactions. In real film, red/green/blue sensitive layers affect each other during exposure and development. This models that chemical crosstalk.

Print Stock

19 print stocks from Kodak Vision, ECP, Fuji Eterna-RDI, and Crystal Archive families. Each has its own gamma, color timing response, and density range that shapes the final look.

Printer Points

RGB timing light controls—0-50 scale where 25 is neutral. Emulates the light valves on an optical printer. One point equals roughly 1/12 stop. Industry-standard color balancing method.

Physical Halation

Light passes through emulsion, bounces off the film base, and re-exposes from behind. Creates the organic red glow around bright sources. Film gauge controls scatter size—16mm spreads wider, 65mm stays tighter.

Film Grain

Density-dependent grain that responds to exposure like real silver halide crystals. Coarser in shadows, finer in highlights. Film gauge scales particle size—16mm is grittier, 65mm is smoother.

Helix

Texture & surface character

Helix is a look-development solution focused on texture, structure, and surface character.

It is designed to refine how an image feels. Helix shapes micro-contrast and perceived detail in a controlled manner. The result is depth and tactility that reads naturally across formats and delivery specs.

Clarity

Local contrast via adaptive unsharp mask. A large-radius kernel isolates tonal structure from texture. The difference is scaled and added back, weighted by a luminance mask that attenuates in highlights and shadows to prevent clipping.

Acutance

Edge slope enhancement. Computed as the first derivative of luminance, amplified at tonal boundaries. Unlike resolution-based sharpening, acutance affects perceived crispness without adding high-frequency content or halo artifacts.

Spatial Equalizer

Laplacian pyramid decomposition into discrete frequency bands. Each octave is gain-adjusted independently—attenuate high frequencies to reduce skin texture, boost mid frequencies to define form. Reconstruction preserves DC and avoids phase shift.

Phi

Shot-level balance

Phi is a shot-level solution designed for colorists who want speed without compromise.

It consolidates exposure, contrast shaping, color separation, density, and perceptual balance into a single, coherent toolset. Instead of stacking nodes and fighting interactions, Phi lets you shape an image holistically—one pass, one intent, one result.

Printer Lights

RGB offset controls that emulate timing light adjustments on an optical printer. Balances the image before any other processing occurs.

Exposure

Stop-based gain applied in linear light. Converts to linear, multiplies by 2^exposure, then back to log—preserving highlight and shadow detail through the transform.

Contrast

Piecewise-power sigmoid centered on ACEScct mid-gray (0.414). Anchors black and white endpoints while expanding or compressing everything in between.

Shadows / Midtones / Highlights

Zone-weighted offsets in log space. Shadows use smoothstep falloff below 0.25, midtones use a Gaussian bell at mid-gray, highlights ramp above 0.55.

Black Point / White Point

Toe and shoulder curves with flat plateaus. Black point lifts shadows to a new floor with power-curve blending. White point compresses highlights down with the same math mirrored.

Toe & Shoulder

Explicit curve shaping below toe pivot and above shoulder pivot. Strength and length parameters give independent control of roll-off steepness and range.

Saturation

OKLab-based chroma scaling. Converts to perceptually uniform space, scales the a/b chromatic channels, converts back. Uniform perceived intensity change across all hues.

Vibrance

Selective saturation that protects skin tones and already-saturated colors. Detects skin by positive OKLab 'a' values, weights boost inversely to existing chroma.

Skin: Melanin & Hemoglobin

Signed-quadratic response curves. Melanin shifts toward yellow-brown (more) or away (less). Hemoglobin controls the red-green axis—more for ruddier skin, less for golden tones.

Skin: Richness & Density

Richness is skin-isolated saturation with a soft S-curve. Density is luminance shift localized to warm pixels. Both auto-mask based on r > g > b warmth detection.

Skin Texture

Clarity-style local contrast applied only to skin tones. 16-sample blur at two radii extracts detail, then midtone-weighted gain enhances or softens texture without affecting the rest of the image.

Tetrahedral Secondaries

Per-hue HSV adjustment for red, green, blue, cyan, magenta, and yellow. Tetrahedral interpolation blends smoothly between adjacent vertices—no banding at hue boundaries.

Alpha

Film-Negative Transform

Alpha defines the working space. It moves images into and out of film-negative domains, enabling printer-light style balancing and exposure-driven shot control.

Direction

Two modes: Digital → Negative converts camera footage into film-negative density space. Negative → Digital inverts the transform for output. Chain two instances to work entirely in negative domain.

Source Color Space

Declares camera input gamut—ARRI AWG4/AWG3, Sony SGamut3, Panasonic VGamut, RED RWG, DaVinci Wide Gamut, ACES AP0/AP1, Canon Cinema, Blackmagic Wide Gamut, Rec.709, or Rec.2020. All conversions route through AP1 as a clean interchange space.

Source Gamma

Specifies camera log curve—LogC4, LogC3, S-Log3, V-Log, Log3G10, Canon Log 2/3, BMD Film Gen5, N-Log, F-Log, F-Log2, ACEScct, DaVinci Intermediate, or Rec.709. Linearization happens before any gamut math.

Digital Color Space

The working gamut for grading. Typically matches timeline color science. Same options as Source—pick once, keep consistent across the session.

Digital Gamma

Working transfer function. ACEScct and DaVinci Intermediate provide grading-friendly midtone density. Linear for compositing. Rec.709 for legacy workflows.

Negative Gamma

Film stock density curves—Cineon standard, Kodak Aim, Carlos Aim, Extended range, Soft highlight roll, Contrast for punchier shadows, or ADX for Academy workflows. Each models how a specific stock encodes exposure into density.
